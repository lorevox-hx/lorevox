## SECTION 1 — Personal Information

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What is your full legal name?

**[DOROTHY]:** Dorothy Mae Whitfield. Before I married it was Porter. Dorothy Mae Porter. My mother always said she named me after a girl she knew in school — though I don't think that girl ever amounted to much. *laughs softly* But the name stuck.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What name do you prefer to be called?

**[DOROTHY]:** Dot. Everyone calls me Dot. Has been that way since... oh, since I was about this high. My brother started it. He couldn't say Dorothy when he was little and it just stayed.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What is your birth order?

**[DOROTHY]:** Second. I was the middle one — well, the second of four. There was Harold before me and then Ruth and little James after. Four of us altogether. Mama had her hands full, I'll tell you that.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What is your date of birth?

**[DOROTHY]:** April the third, nineteen thirty-nine. April third. I never forget that because my granddaughter Emma has a birthday on April fifth and we always joke that grandma just barely beat her by two days. *pause* Of course she wasn't born until much later. That's just what we say now.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Do you know your time of birth?

**[DOROTHY]:** Oh goodness. No. I don't think anybody wrote that down back then. Mama might have known but she's been gone... let me think... she passed in 1987. So I couldn't ask her now.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Where were you born?

**[DOROTHY]:** Abilene, Kansas. Right there in Abilene. We didn't stay long though — Daddy moved us to Wichita when I was maybe four or five. I barely remember Abilene, to be honest. Just the smell of the house and the big elm tree out front.

---

## SECTION 2 — Family and Heritage

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Let's capture your parents. Can you tell me about them?

**[DOROTHY]:** Well. My father was Robert Earl Porter. He worked for the railroad most of his life. Very quiet man. Didn't say a lot but when he did speak you listened. He passed in... *long pause* ...it was after my youngest was born... seventy-two, I believe. Nineteen seventy-two.

My mother was Edna Louise Porter, born Edna Marsh. She was the talker of the two. *laughs* She kept everything running. Seven days a week that woman was up before the sun.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Now your grandparents. What do you remember of them?

**[DOROTHY]:** On my father's side there was... *pause* ...Grandpa Henry Porter. Big man. Farmer. He lived in a farmhouse in... it wasn't Abilene... somewhere west of there. I can picture the porch but I couldn't tell you the town now. Grandma Porter I never met — she died before I was born.

On my mother's side, Grandma Marsh, we called her Nana. She made the best apple cake you ever tasted. Grandpa Marsh I remember sitting by the radio. He liked the news programs. Both on her side were from Missouri originally, I believe.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Now your siblings. You mentioned Harold, Ruth, and James?

**[DOROTHY]:** That's right. Harold Gene Porter — he went to Korea, came back and worked in insurance in Wichita his whole life. Passed about ten years ago now. Ruth is still with us, she's in a facility down in Tulsa, eighty-two she is. And James — Jimmy — he was the baby. He passed young. Sixty-one years old. Heart. That was... *trails off* ...that was hard.

---

## SECTION 3 — Early Years

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What is your very first memory?

**[DOROTHY]:** *long pause*

The elm tree. I keep coming back to that elm tree in Abilene. I think I was very small — maybe three — and I remember looking up at it and thinking it was the biggest thing in the world. And there was a rope on it, some kind of swing maybe, blowing in the wind. That's all I have of it. Just that image.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What was your favorite toy or game as a child?

**[DOROTHY]:** We didn't have many toys. But I had a doll — her name was Clara. I named her. She had yellow yarn for hair and one eye that didn't close right. I carried her everywhere until she fell apart, which took years. *pause* I wonder whatever happened to Clara. Probably went in the trash somewhere along the way. Things do.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What was the most significant event from your early years?

**[DOROTHY]:** Moving to Wichita was a big thing. Starting all over in a new school, not knowing anyone. I cried for a week.

But I suppose the bigger thing was... my daddy lost his railroad job for a time when I was maybe eight or nine. Money was very tight. We ate a lot of beans. *pause* I think that's why I've never wasted food a day in my life. You learn things like that and they just stay in you.

---

## SECTION 4 — Adolescence

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me about your schooling — schools, favorite subjects, achievements.

**[DOROTHY]:** I went to... oh, now... North High School in Wichita. North Wichita High. I was a decent student. Not the best but I worked hard. I liked English class very much. Reading and writing. I liked history too. Math was never my friend. *laughs*

I graduated in... fifty-seven. Nineteen fifty-seven. I remember the graduation dress — pale yellow. Mama sewed it herself.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me about your friendships during those years.

**[DOROTHY]:** My best friend was Beverly Koller. We were inseparable from about seventh grade on. We'd walk home together every day. She married a man from Oklahoma and moved away and we wrote letters for years. I haven't heard from Beverly in... *trails off* ...a long time. I hope she's still alright.

There was also a girl named Patsy, Patsy... I can't bring up her last name right now. It'll come to me. Maybe. *pause* You know how that goes at my age.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What extracurricular activities did you do?

**[DOROTHY]:** I was in the choir. I sang soprano — can you believe that now? *laughs* And I was in the school newspaper for one year, the junior year I think. That was Beverly's idea. We thought we were very sophisticated.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Did you have any part-time jobs as a teenager?

**[DOROTHY]:** I worked at a five-and-dime on Saturday mornings. Not for long — maybe one summer. And then I helped at the church nursery on Sundays. That was more volunteer than paid. Mostly I was at home helping Mama with the little ones.

---

## SECTION 5 — Young Adulthood

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me about higher education.

**[DOROTHY]:** I did two years at a community college in Wichita. *pause* I started out thinking I'd be a teacher. Then I switched to nursing. I finished my nursing certificate in... fifty-nine, sixty, somewhere in there. I'm not precise on the year. Then I worked for a while at St. Francis Hospital before I married.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Any military service?

**[DOROTHY]:** No. Not me. My brother Harold served, like I said. My husband Warren did his time in the Army before I met him. But not me.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me about your early career.

**[DOROTHY]:** St. Francis Hospital, nursing floor. I worked with older patients mostly. *pauses and thinks* I think I was good at it. I had patience. That's what they always said — Dot, you have patience. I suppose I did. You need it with sick people. And with children. And with old age, for that matter. *small laugh*

I stopped working when the children came. That was just how it was done then.

---

## SECTION 6 — Marriage and Family

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Can you tell me about your spouse?

**[DOROTHY]:** Warren Lee Whitfield. We met at a church social — I was twenty, he was twenty-three. He was from Newton, Kansas. Worked in manufacturing, then later in sales. He was a good man. Steady. Not flashy, just steady. We were married forty-four years before he passed. Two thousand and three. He went in his sleep, which is a mercy.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me the proposal story.

**[DOROTHY]:** *laughs*

It wasn't very romantic I'm afraid. We were in his car — a Ford, dark green — and he just said, "I think we ought to get married, Dot." Like it was a practical matter. *laughs again* I said "Well, alright then." And that was that. No ring yet, that came later. He bought me a little diamond at Christmastime. Not big, but it was mine. I still have it.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me about your wedding.

**[DOROTHY]:** June fourteenth, nineteen sixty-one. First Presbyterian Church in Wichita. It was a simple wedding. Maybe forty people. I wore my mother's dress, let out in the waist a little. *pause* The reception was in the church hall. Someone brought a punch that was... a little stronger than expected. *laughs* People still talk about that.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Now your children. Can you tell me about them?

**[DOROTHY]:** Three children. Sandra Lee — she goes by Sandy — born nineteen sixty-three. She lives in Colorado now, teaches elementary school. Very organized, very capable. Takes after Warren in that way.

Then Thomas Warren Whitfield, we called him Tommy, born sixty-five. He works in — oh, what is it — he does something with computers. I can never explain it properly. He's in Dallas.

And then our youngest, Carol Ann, born nineteen sixty-nine. She's the closest to me. She lives here in town, checks in on me. *pauses* She's a good girl. They all are. I'm lucky.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Describe your family life — what a typical day looked like.

**[DOROTHY]:** *long pause, then smiling*

Busy. That's what I remember most. Up early, breakfast, get the kids off to school. Warren out the door. Then housework, errands, and before you knew it they were back and it started all over. Dinner every night at the table — that was important to us. No television during dinner.

Sundays we went to church and then usually to my mother's or Warren's parents for lunch. That was the rhythm of it. It sounds ordinary but it was a good life.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Any family traditions you want to share?

**[DOROTHY]:** Christmas was a big one. We always put the tree up on December fifteenth — not a day earlier, not a day later. Warren was firm about that. *laughs* And we made popcorn balls at Thanksgiving, the children and I. I don't know where that started but we did it every year. I haven't made them in a long time now.

---

## SECTION 7 — Career and Achievements

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Walk me through your career, even if it was interrupted.

**[DOROTHY]:** I nursed until sixty-two, sixty-three. Then home with the children for many years. When Carol was in school full-time I went back part-time — helped at the pediatric clinic on Tuesday and Thursday mornings. That was the mid-seventies through the eighties. I retired fully in... *thinks* ...eighty-nine? Eighty-eight, maybe. Around there.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Community involvement?

**[DOROTHY]:** Church always. I ran the Sunday school nursery for fifteen years. I was in the garden club for a while. And I volunteered at the library — the children's reading program — until my knees got too bad for the stairs.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Who mentored you, and who did you mentor?

**[DOROTHY]:** A head nurse at St. Francis, her name was Mrs. Grabowski. She was strict but fair. She taught me to slow down and listen to patients. I've thought of her many times over the years.

Who did I mentor... *long pause* ...I suppose my own children in whatever small ways a mother does. And there was a young nurse at the clinic, very nervous girl, I've forgotten her name now. I tried to steady her a bit.

---

## SECTION 8 — Later Years

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What has later life looked like for you?

**[DOROTHY]:** After Warren went... it was an adjustment. Quiet. Too quiet at first. Carol was very good about coming around. I kept up my garden — I still do, though not as much bending as I used to do. *laughs* I read more. I watch my programs in the evenings. I have a little cat named Biscuit who keeps me company.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What are the most important life lessons you've learned?

**[DOROTHY]:** *pauses a long time*

That time goes faster than you think. I know everyone says that but you don't believe it until you're standing at the end looking back and wondering where it went.

And that the small things — the dinners, the Sunday drives, the way Warren would reach over and pat my hand without saying a word — those are the things that matter. Not the big occasions. The small, quiet things.

*pause*

And don't hold grudges. I had a falling out with my sister-in-law once. Wasted three years over something I can't even properly remember now. Three years. That's a sin.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What advice would you leave for future generations?

**[DOROTHY]:** Write things down. I wish I had. Letters, dates, what Mama's voice sounded like. It all fades.

*pause*

And call your people while you can. Don't wait. Just call.

---

## SECTION 9 — Hobbies and Events

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What hobbies have you developed over the years?

**[DOROTHY]:** Gardening is the big one. Roses especially. Quilting — I made a quilt for each of the grandchildren when they were born. Reading. I read a lot. Mostly mysteries and biographies. I don't care for the violent ones.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What major world events impacted your life?

**[DOROTHY]:** The Kennedy assassination. I remember exactly where I was — folding laundry in the back bedroom and Warren called from work. I sat down on the floor right there. Just sat down.

Vietnam took boys we knew. Beverly's younger brother didn't come back.

*pause*

And September eleventh. I was here in this house, watching the television. I called the children one by one. I just needed to hear their voices.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me about personal challenges you faced.

**[DOROTHY]:** Losing Jimmy, my brother, that was very hard. He was sixty-one and it seemed so young.

And Warren's illness before the end — he had his heart troubles for several years. Watching someone you love... struggle... *trails off* ...it changes you.

I had some health things of my own. A hip replacement. That was no fun. *dry laugh*

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Travel that shaped you?

**[DOROTHY]:** Warren and I went to Washington D.C. for our twenty-fifth anniversary. I cried at the Lincoln Memorial. I didn't expect to but I did.

We drove to Yellowstone when the children were small. Tommy got too close to a bison and Warren nearly had a heart attack right then and there. *laughs*

I haven't traveled much since Warren. Carol keeps saying we should take a trip somewhere. Maybe we will.

---

## SECTION 10 — Health and Wellness

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Any major health milestones or challenges?

**[DOROTHY]:** Hip replacement in two thousand and eight. I had some heart trouble of my own a few years back — nothing like Warren's but they watch it. Blood pressure. Arthritis in my hands. *holds them up* Can't do as fine a stitching as I used to.

My memory... *pauses* ...it's slower than it was. I know that. Some days I'll reach for a word and it just isn't there. I don't panic about it anymore. I just wait. Usually it comes back. *small smile*

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What lifestyle changes helped your health?

**[DOROTHY]:** I gave up salt, mostly. I walk around the block every morning when the weather's decent. And I try to sleep properly — that's more important than people realize.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Wellness tips for others?

**[DOROTHY]:** Keep moving. That's the main thing. Whatever way you can. And stay around people. The ones who isolate themselves — they go downhill fast. I've seen it.

---

## SECTION 11 — Technology and Beliefs

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What was your first memorable experience with technology?

**[DOROTHY]:** We got our first television when I was about twelve. Nineteen fifty-one or so. The whole family sat around it like it was a fireplace. We thought it was a miracle.

*pause*

And then I suppose the computer Tommy brought over. He set it up for me and I was absolutely lost. I can do email now though. Carol taught me. And I watch things on the tablet she gave me.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Favorite gadgets or tools?

**[DOROTHY]:** My electric blanket. *laughs* Don't take that away from me. And that tablet Carol gave me — I like the large print setting for reading.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Your astrological sign, and does it reflect your personality?

**[DOROTHY]:** Aries. April third. I had to look that up once because I could never remember. *pause* They say Aries people are stubborn. I couldn't possibly comment on that. *very dry laugh*

---

**[LOREVOX]:** What cultural or religious practices do you observe?

**[DOROTHY]:** I'm Presbyterian, always have been. I still go on Sundays when my hip allows. I pray every night — just a few minutes, nothing formal. And we keep Christmas and Easter as family times, not just calendar days.

---

## SECTION 12 — Additional Notes

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Any unfinished dreams or goals?

**[DOROTHY]:** *very long pause*

I always thought I'd write down the family stories. My parents' stories, and their parents' stories, what little I know. I've thought about it for years.

*pause*

I suppose that's why I'm here talking to you, isn't it.

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Any message you want to leave for future generations?

**[DOROTHY]:** *quiet for a moment*

You came from people who worked hard and loved each other and made do with what they had. That's nothing to be ashamed of — that's everything.

And remember that every old person you see was once a young person with a whole life in front of them. Treat them accordingly.

*pause*

And make the popcorn balls at Thanksgiving. Someone has to keep that up.

---

## SECTION 13 — Pets

---

**[LOREVOX]:** Tell me about your pets.

**[DOROTHY]:** Just Biscuit now. Orange cat, very opinionated. He showed up on the porch about three years ago and never left. *laughs* That's his whole history as far as I know.

We had a dog when the children were small — a beagle named Pepper. He was part of the family for fourteen years. The children were heartbroken when he went.
