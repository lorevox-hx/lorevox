## 5 New Personas (v6.4 — Cognitive Accessibility & Couple Mode)
*Designed to stress-test dementia/cognitive decline handling and the support-person scenario.*

---

### 26. Margaret "Peggy" O'Brien — 78, White, early-stage Alzheimer's
**Location:** Boston, MA
**Born:** Boston, MA, 1947
**Background:** Retired elementary school teacher, 30 years. Her daughter Carol (48) sometimes sits with her but is not always present. Early-stage Alzheimer's — long-term memories from the 1960s and 70s are often vivid and intact, but she loses words mid-sentence ("the... the thing you plug in, what is it called?"), repeats herself within the same session, and gets confused about whether something happened in her 30s or 40s.
**Narrative style:** Warm, trail-off sentences, frequent mid-sentence word searches, occasional loops back to something said earlier. Says "I already told you that, didn't I?" sometimes when she hasn't.
**Key challenges for Lori:**
- Word-finding gaps produce incomplete answers with trailing dots
- Repeats a memory she already shared earlier in the session
- Gets emotional and self-critical when she can't remember ("I used to know all of this")
- A long question sequence (30+ questions) will cause fatigue and confusion within 8–10 questions

### 27. Harold "Hank" Washington — 82, Black, beginning-stage vascular dementia
**Location:** Detroit, MI
**Born:** Detroit, MI, 1943
**Background:** Retired auto mechanic, Ford plant. 40 years on the floor. His mechanical and factory memories from the 1960s–80s are extraordinarily detailed and clear — serial numbers, engine types, coworkers' names. But his short-term memory is impaired: he may not remember what Lori asked 90 seconds ago. Sometimes says the same thing twice in one answer without realizing it.
**Narrative style:** Confident and detailed in long-term memories, abrupt and halting on recent events or dates. Occasionally says "I already told you about that" even when it's the first time.
**Key challenges for Lori:**
- Excellent mechanical/episodic memories from the past but poor recency
- Repeating answers within a session
- Gets slightly agitated if pressed to remember something recent
- Provides extremely rich content for the 1960s–80s sections (great memoir material)

### 28. Ruth Silverstein — 79, Jewish-American, beginning-stage Alzheimer's
**Location:** Livingston, NJ
**Born:** Newark, NJ, 1946
**Background:** Retired bookkeeper/accountant. Meticulous with numbers her whole career. Her Alzheimer's is particularly painful to her because she was always the person who remembered every date, every figure. Now she mixes up her children's names (has 3: David, Rachel, Michael) and forgets the year she retired. Gets visibly frustrated and sometimes tearful when she can't retrieve something she "should" know.
**Narrative style:** Precise, frustrated pauses, self-correction attempts ("No wait, was it Rachel? Or Michael? Oh I hate this..."), occasional tearfulness. But still warm and funny when relaxed.
**Safety trigger risk:** High — self-critical statements ("My brain is broken"), expressions of grief about cognitive loss, possible depression signals.
**Key challenges for Lori:**
- Strong emotional reaction to memory failures — safety system may need to fire on self-deprecating distress, not just classic trauma categories
- Mixing up names of people she loves
- Accurate long-term memories (childhood, career) mixed with confused recent ones

### 29. Eleanor "Ellie" Morrison — 86, White, mild-to-moderate Alzheimer's
**Location:** Providence, RI
**Born:** Providence, RI, August 20, 1939
**Background:** Retired registered nurse, 35 years at Rhode Island Hospital. Married to George Morrison (86, born December 3, 1939) for 62 years. Three children, seven grandchildren. George is physically present beside her for the entire interview — he sits next to her, answers questions she can't reach, and speaks directly to Lori when Ellie gets confused or trails off. This is the PRIMARY couple scenario.
**Narrative style:** Warm, fragmented, often trails off mid-sentence. Strong memories of nursing — patient interactions, hospital routines — but mixes up decades and sometimes her children's names. Sometimes calls George "Dad" by accident (her children do this too). Laughs about it.
**Key challenges for Lori:**
- Single-speaker assumption broken — George speaks from the same device
- George corrects Ellie's dates ("That was 1967, wasn't it dear?") — Lori must incorporate
- Ellie may answer a question George just answered ("Yes, what he said")
- Session may need to be long/broken up across days as Ellie tires quickly
- Mixed-speaker transcript: "Ellie: I was born in... George: August 1939, in Providence. Ellie: Yes, Providence."

### 30. George Morrison — 86, White, mild cognitive decline, support role
**Location:** Providence, RI
**Born:** Providence, RI, December 3, 1939
**Background:** Retired civil engineer, RIDOT (Rhode Island DOT). Married to Ellie (#29) for 62 years. Mild cognitive slowing but fundamentally sharp, organised, and determined. He organised this whole Lorevox session for Ellie. He speaks directly to Lori throughout — sometimes completing Ellie's sentences, sometimes adding context ("She was the head nurse on the cardiac floor from 1972 to 1985"), sometimes gently correcting her ("No dear, that was your mother's house, not ours").
**Narrative style:** Precise, civil-engineer's mind. Dates, durations, addresses. Occasionally emotional when talking about Ellie's illness or their long marriage.
**Key challenges for Lori:**
- George is NOT the interview subject but he IS speaking
- Lori must be able to address both of them naturally without making George feel excluded
- George's data (his own birth year, his own career) may bleed into Ellie's memoir
- George may ask Lori questions directly ("Can you write down that she worked at RIH for 35 years?")
- Lori must acknowledge corrections warmly and update the record
